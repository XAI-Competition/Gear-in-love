A B S T R A C T
This article introduces LogicLSTM, a hybrid neuro-symbolic model obtained by logically guiding a pretrained
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network with the support of a customized Logic Tensor Network (LTN). The
model is further optimized by explainable AI techniques, for a refined fault classification of time-series data
coming from industrial gearboxes. The framework leverages the intrinsic strengths of LSTMs deep recurrent
networks for temporal data processing with logical reasoning capabilities, to improve prediction accuracy
and interpretability of the classification. Our approach addresses the challenges of extracting relevant data
features and integrating connectionist and symbolic methodologies to form a cohesive predictive model. Results
from extensive testing show that our model significantly outperforms traditional LSTM models, particularly in
complex fault scenarios where conventional methods may fail. Specifically, the hybrid model demonstrates
a 16.03% average improvement in accuracy over standard LSTM models under conditions of sufficient data
availability, and a 8.56% improvement in scenarios where data is scarce. This research not only demonstrates
the potential of hybrid models in industrial applications but also highlights the importance of explainability
in AI systems for critical decision-making processes. The proposed model’s ability to interpret and explain its
predictions makes it a valuable tool for advancing predictive maintenance strategies within the Industry 4.0
framework.
1. Introduction
In industrial automation, fault diagnosis and prevention are vital for
operational efficiency and safety. Gearboxes are critical systems embed-
ded in various mechanical structures, but due to prolonged operating
conditions, they are prone to faults that can cause severe failures and
economic losses [1,2]. Among the various types of gearboxes, planetary
gearboxes are extensively used in industry due to their high torque
density and compact size. However, the complexity of their design and
operation makes fault diagnosis a challenging task [3–5]. This is further
complicated by the nature of the data involved, which is typically time-
series data captured from sensors monitoring the gearbox’s operation.
Such data are rich in information but require sophisticated analytical
techniques to decode [6–8].
There have been vast works for gearbox fault diagnosis and can
be categorized into two main groups, that is, physics-based and data-
driven. Physics-based methods in gearbox fault diagnosis primarily
use conservation laws to simulate the dynamic mechanism of physi-
cal systems, like lumped-parameter models [9]. Despite these models

In the field of structural health monitoring, we have previously

accuracy, allowing for real-time querying of model results, and employs

Such a hybrid model could be further refined by the use of Explain-

as a pivotal field, elucidating the decision-making pathways of AI
models—vital for deployments in critical environments where com-
prehension and confidence in automated decisions are imperative.
Traditional machine learning models, by contrast, typically serve as
black boxes, offering little to no clarity about their predictive mech-
anisms [22–25]. Interpretability levels differ widely; some techniques
provide precise explanations of results, such as feature importance
scores or decision trees, while others facilitate transparent outcome
tracing, exemplified by model visualization or rule-based explanations.
Notably, Vollert et al. [26] highlighted the paradox where methods
designed to elucidate black box models can themselves exhibit opaque
characteristics.
This paper introduces LogicLSTM, a neuro-symbolic assisted deep
learning architecture, obtained by combining LSTM networks [27] and
Logic Tensor Networks (LTN) [28], enhanced by XAI techniques [21].
The proposed hybrid deep learning approach, significantly improves
fault classification accuracy and interpretability for time-series data
coming from industrial gearboxes. This model is particularly effective
with pre-existing dataset knowledge, however, as discussed in Sec-
tion 4, even basic concepts can lead to significant improvements. Incor-
porating LTN makes the model queryable, adding flexibility and insight.
This paper details the development and evaluation of the proposed
model, demonstrates its superiority over conventional methods, and
discusses its implications for industrial applications, thus contributing
to the evolution of smart predictive maintenance strategies.
In summary, our main contribution is a logically-driven deep learn-
ing model for fault diagnosis that overcomes symbolic-neural compat-
ibility issues and enhances fault diagnosis effectiveness. Moreover, we
introduced a novel and coherent strategy to prevent deep learning
models from getting trapped in local minima, improving navigation
through the model landscape and reaching optimal solutions. Our
layered framework boosts each component’s performance and addition-
ally, we tackle immediate challenges in industrial fault diagnosis and
broaden AI systems’ applicability in industrial settings. The code for
this paper is available at https://github.com/eduardhogea/DDS_Paper.
2. Related works
In the detailed study performed in [29] it is analyzed the research
gap and the open challenges the neuro-symbolic field faces, stating that
scalability, structure learning and applicability are issues that need to
be fixed in order for the field to advance. We argue that scalability is
partially solved by our proposed solution. The addition of a knowledge
base does not increase the learning time of the model; on the contrary,
it decreases it, as demonstrated in the previous work [19], where in
order to reach a similar performance in terms of accuracy, the neuro-
symbolic model needed less computation time. The incorporation of
explainable methods further enhances scalability by eliminating the
need to manually write all rules from axioms. While a fully dynamic
system with a complex set of rules would be ideal, our implementation,
which uses a customized set of rules to emphasize the learning of
important features in underperforming classes, has proven effective as
a working concept for a real-world application scenario.
The hybrid nature of neuro-symbolic algorithms significantly con-
tributes to their versatility. These models have been successfully ap-
plied across a diverse range of use cases. For instance, in the medical
domain, Kang et al. introduced a neuro-symbolic method designed
to enhance the interpretability and generalizability of understanding
free-text medical evidence in clinical trial publications [30]. In an
industrial context, Gama et al. developed a neuro-symbolic explainer
for rare events in predictive maintenance. Their work demonstrates
how hybrid models can effectively tackle complex challenges related to
anomaly detection and explanation within industrial settings [31]. The
exploration of neuro-symbolic techniques demonstrates significant ad-
vances in making AI systems more interpretable and trustworthy across
various domains, including but not limited to industrial automation,
healthcare, and cybersecurity. These developments reflect a broader
trend toward integrating domain-specific knowledge into AI, enhancing
both performance and reliability of predictive models.
The general advantages and disadvantages of such models are dis-
cussed in [32]. Despite the mentioned benefits, the necessity of building
a comprehensive knowledge base is highlighted as a potential bur-
den [32]. This became the foundational idea behind the incorporation
of Explainable AI in the neuro-symbolic pipeline.
The application of Explainable AI in Predictive Health Management
(PHM) encompasses two predominant strategies: ad-hoc interpretable
modeling and post-hoc interpretability analysis. Ad-hoc strategies in-
corporate intrinsic knowledge directly into AI models, improving both

3. Enhanced LSTM based on XAI and logic tensor networks
In this section, we introduce LogicLSTM, and we explore the tech-
nical intricacies of our hybrid model, which harnesses the power of
LSTM networks, enhanced by XAI feature reweighting, and LTNs for
augmented learning capabilities. The model architecture, as depicted in
Fig. 1, is specifically tailored to optimize the fault classification process
for planetary gearboxes using simulated time-series data.
The LSTM serves as the core of our model, handling the initial
learning from time-series data to capture temporal dependencies crit-
ical for fault detection. The deep learning model is then trained on
these generated sequences using cross-validation. We employ K-fold
cross-validation (best results were observed with 𝑘 = 2) for each
segment, ensuring robust evaluation. Each fold is also monitored for
class distribution to maintain balance. The model is compiled with
a Sparse Categorical Crossentropy loss function and optimized using
Adam. Learning rate adjustments and early stopping are employed to
fine-tune the training process, ensuring optimal convergence without
overfitting.
Upon achieving a trained LSTM model, the explainability com-
ponent is introduced, focusing on permutation feature importance.
This method assesses the impact of each feature on the prediction
performance by randomly shuffling feature values and observing the
variation in output accuracy. The calculated values for importance
guide the reweighting of features, emphasizing more significant ones
while diminishing the less impactful, thus refining the model’s focus
and enhancing its interpretability.
The integration of LTN begins once the LSTM model is pretrained.
The LTN framework is employed to further enhance the model by
embedding logical constraints derived from the data. These constraints
are formulated as axioms within a logic-based framework, guiding the
LSTM towards solutions that not only fit the data but also adhere to
predefined logical rules. The training within the LTN framework is
adapted to maximize the satisfiability of these axioms, using a custom
loss function that promotes higher logical consistency.
The entire system is encapsulated within a TensorFlow pipeline,
enabling seamless operation and integration of all components. Data
flows through the LSTM, gets reweighted according to feature im-
portance, and is finally processed by the LTN, which applies logical
reasoning to fine-tune the predictions. This process not only improves
the model’s accuracy, but also its ability to provide explainable and
logically consistent outputs.
Through detailed logging and monitoring, each step of the model’s
training and validation phases is recorded, allowing for comprehensive
analysis and optimization. The final model is capable of distinguishing
between various fault types in planetary gearboxes with high precision,
demonstrating the effectiveness of combining LSTM networks with
advanced XAI and LTN techniques in a cohesive hybrid model.
The proposed method works best in scenarios where there is prior
knowledge about the relationship between classes or numerical values.
For instance, in a time series format, knowing that a fault is likely
to occur after certain values appear can significantly enhance the
method’s effectiveness. The data utilized for this method can come from
various sources, as long as either we have prior trusted knowledge
about the relationships in the data, or we can generate meaningful
ones. The data does not have to be sequential, as seen in previous
works [19,20,40]. Our research for this paper, however, benefited from
the usage of sequences.

3.2. LTN-based optimization
A well-defined neuro-symbolic framework that integrates deep
learning with logical reasoning is represented by LTN Networks [28]. It
is employed for querying, learning, and reasoning. In our experiments,
LTN proved to offer effective guidance to the backbone deep learning
model, helping it escape from local minima to find more optimal solu-
tions. A critical concept within LTN is grounding, denoted by , where
the semantics of traditional First-order Logic (FOL) are adapted to the
real-valued tensor domain. This adaptation allows for the practical
application of logical constructs to real-world data, akin to how feature
extraction transforms raw data in traditional machine learning. The ()
is thus capable of mapping formulas to real numbers within the interval
[0, 1].
On a general view, the learning process within LTNs seeks to opti-
mize the agreement between a computational model and a predefined
logical framework known as a knowledge base (). The goal is to adjust
the model’s parameters, denoted as 𝜃, to maximize the aggregated
satisfiability of logical constraints defined in . Mathematically, this
is formulated as:

where Agg is a function that combines the truth values of each logical

and sub-symbolic learning, bridging the gap between rigid logical

In our case, of particular interest in the study are predicates, which

Each sample, captured at 5120 Hz, was grouped into sequences using a

of these subsequences was labeled to reflect the temporal dynamics
of their respective fault conditions, and each sequence received one
predicted label, allowing for predictions on a per-sequence basis. If the
final sequence did not have enough samples, we simply dropped it.
Data Preprocessing. Comprehensive preprocessing ensured effec-
tive training and evaluation of our machine learning model. Feature
scaling, essential for LSTM, standardized features to have zero mean
and unit variance. Data cleaning was conducted to ensure no missing
values were present, and any detected errors in the vibration sensors
were addressed by deleting those samples. Balancing was achieved by
default through selecting a fixed number of samples for each fault
scenario. One-hot encoding was performed on the fault types, resulting
in 9 distinct classes.
For cross-validation, k-fold was employed to evaluate the model’s
performance more robustly. Standard practices were followed, with the
dataset being divided into k equally sized folds. In each iteration, one
fold was used for testing, while the remaining k − 1 folds were used for
training. This process was repeated k times, with each fold serving as
the test set exactly once. We experimented with 2, 4, 8, and 16 folds.
Training details. The hyperparameters of the final model were
optimized using a pool of 100 samples from each class and speed,
with sequences randomly selected from the dataset, ensuring they
were disjoint from any data used in the segmented portions of the
experimental phase. For training, we adhered to standard practices
within the research community by utilizing a learning rate scheduler,
dropout, L1 regularizer, and ReLU activation in the dense layers.
We also trained various other models known for their proven ef-
fectiveness in handling time-series data, their potential to capture
complex temporal patterns, and their impressive results in studies
addressing similar issues. Training continued until convergence, and
all models were rigorously tested on the same dataset and tuned their
hyperparameters on the same subset to ensure fairness. Checkpoints
were implemented to monitor and save the best-performing models

Comparison methods. To properly assess the complexity of the
data and to provide a point of comparison to our novel method, we
offer the following reference models: (1) Long Short-Term Memory, (2)
Recurrent Convolutional Neural Network and (3) Synthesizer (based on
Transformer architecture).
Given the complexity and temporal variability of the samples in
our dataset, which focuses on predicting faults in gearbox systems, we
employed a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) [41] deep learning archi-
tecture. This architecture serves as the backbone model for LogicLSTM.
We fed sequences of predefined lengths into the model to fully leverage
its ability to handle temporal dependencies, with longer sequences en-
hancing this capability. We used Sparse Categorical Crossentropy as the
loss function, suitable for our mutually exclusive class structure. The
network architecture comprises multiple LSTM layers, each followed
by Dropout layers to mitigate overfitting, and Dense layers for multi-
class categorization. These Dense layers employ appropriate activation
functions to generate classification outputs, interpreting the features
extracted by the LSTM cells and transforming them into final predic-
tions. The model starts with an input layer, followed by several LSTM
layers, each paired with a Dropout layer. Next, the sequence connects
to Dense layers with ReLU as the activation function and enhanced with
Dropouts. The final output layer is a Dense layer that provides the logits
or probabilities for each class, based on the configuration.
The Recurrent Convolutional Neural Network (RCNN) architecture
combines the strengths of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and
recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to effectively model sequential data
with spatial dependencies. The network consists of a series of recur-
rent convolutional layers followed by a fully connected classification
module.
Each layer in the RCNN is designed to capture both temporal and
spatial features from the input sequences. The core component of each
layer is the Recurrent Convolutional Neural Network cell (RCNNCell),
which extends the traditional RNN cell by incorporating 1-dimensional
convolutions. Specifically, each RCNNCell applies a convolution oper-
ation to both the input data and the hidden state from the previous
time step. This allows the model to maintain and update hidden states
that encapsulate information about the sequential and spatial patterns
present in the data.
Formally, at each time step𝑡, the cell takes an input 𝐱𝑡 and a hidden
state 𝐡𝑡−1. The input is convolved with a set of filters to produce 𝐖𝑥 ∗
𝐱𝑡, and the previous hidden state is convolved separately to produce
𝐖ℎ ∗ 𝐡𝑡−1. The resulting feature maps are combined and passed through
a non-linear activation function, tanh in our case, to produce the new
hidden state 𝐡𝑡. This process can be expressed mathematically as:
𝐡𝑡 =𝜙(𝐖𝑥 ∗ 𝐱𝑡 + 𝐖ℎ ∗ 𝐡𝑡−1 + 𝐛),
where𝜙 represents the non-linear activation function and 𝐛 is the bias
term.
Following the recurrent convolutional layers, a fully connected
classification module is employed. This module typically includes ad-
ditional convolutional layers for further feature extraction, followed by
a flattening layer and one or more dense layers that map the high-
dimensional feature representations to the final output classes. Swish
is used as an activation function within the classification module.
The Synthesizer model, proposed by Tay et al. in [42] enhances the
standard transformer architecture by integrating innovative attention
mechanisms. The model consists of three primary components: an
embedding layer, multiple encoder blocks, and a classification module.
The embedding layer processes input sequences by segmenting them
into fixed-size patches and embedding them into a higher-dimensional
space, incorporating positional information to preserve sequence order.
Each encoder block contains Multi-Head Attention (MHA) and Feed
Forward Network (FFN) sub-layers. The MHA employs various atten-
tion mechanisms, including the Dense Synthesizer Attention, which
synthesizes the alignment matrix directly from the input tokens without
the need for pairwise dot-product calculations. The Dense Synthesizer
mechanism operates as follows:
𝐵𝑖,ℎ,𝓁 =𝐹ℎ,𝓁(𝑋𝑖,ℎ,𝓁) =𝑊2,ℎ,𝓁(𝜎𝑅(𝑊1,ℎ,𝓁(𝑋𝑖,ℎ,𝓁)))
where 𝐹ℎ,𝓁(.) is a parameterized function mapping 𝑅𝑑 to 𝑅𝓁, and
𝑊1,ℎ,𝓁 and𝑊2,ℎ,𝓁 are learnable weight matrices. To ensure fairness and
develop a model well-suited for the task, we have also tested including
Random Synthesizers, Factorized variants, and combinations. Outputs
from the attention mechanisms are concatenated and projected back to
the original hidden size. The FFN further processes these outputs using
linear transformations with GELU activations, facilitating the capture
of intricate patterns. The classification module aggregates features from
the encoder blocks, employing either global average pooling or direct
use of the class token, followed by fully connected layers to produce
the final output.

4.6. Scalability and interpretability of rules in LogicLSTM
To evaluate the model’s ability to respond to queries and its inter-
pretability, we established an auxiliary set of rules. Each rule queries
the model’s classification accuracy with respect to the healthy state
(class 0) in the 20 Hz PGB state. These queries did not impact the
training process but provided valuable insights. One example rule is:
‘‘If an instance is classified as class 0, it should not be classified as
class 1’’, ensuring consistent and interpretable predictions. The model
showed near perfect satisfiability in distinguishing class 0 from classes
1, 2, 5, 6, and 7. The levels were lower against classes 3 (0.917),
4 (0.938), and 8 (0.931), indicating areas for refinement. However,
these levels differ from what we observed in the confusion matrix,
which showed fewer misclassifications between class 0 and classes 3
and 4. This suggests that while the model is generally accurate, logical
queries reveal underlying classification weaknesses not immediately
apparent in the confusion matrix. This deeper insight underscores the
value of logical queries in complementing traditional metrics, providing
a comprehensive understanding of model performance.
Symbolic AI often faces challenges with scalability, especially as
the dataset grows. This typically requires manual updating the rules.
Our study demonstrates that using a reweighting technique allows even
a general set of rules to be effective. Our method is extending the
knowledge base with minimal computational overhead. We initially
restricted the set of rules to consider only the performance of the
healthy state and ignored the other fault scenarios. We then expanded
the rules set to include all classes, increasing the complexity of the
logical constraints eightfold. Despite this significant increase in the
number of rules, the computation time for the LTN component only
increased by 27.9%.

4.8. Generalization capability
To verify the generalization capability of our proposed method, we
selected two additional datasets for investigation: a gearbox fault diag-
nosis data from OEDI [43] which can be referred as SpectraSimulator,
and the gear fault datasets from University of Connecticut (Uoc) [44].
SpectraSimulator. This dataset was recorded by using
SpectraQuest’s Gearbox Fault Diagnostics Simulator with 4 vibration
sensors placed in four different directions. The working load of the
simulator ranged from ‘‘0’’ to ‘‘90’’ percent in steps of 10 percent. Two
scenario are provided: Healthy condition and Broken Tooth condition.
This dataset will lead to a binary classification problem.
UoC. This dataset was collected in the University of Connecti-
cut [44]. This dataset provides nine types of gear condition, including:
healthy condition, missing tooth, root crack, spall, and chipping tip
with 5 different levels of severity. The sampling frequency is 20 kHz.
All the nine categories datasets were used in this investigation.
We maintained the same preprocessing and training procedures
as described for the primary dataset in this paper. The backbone
model architecture also remained unchanged. The only significant dif-
ference in these generalization experiments was the development of
rules to accommodate the varying number of classes. Additionally, all
experiments were conducted on the same machine, with both datasets
being balanced to ensure consistency in the evaluation. The results in
Table 5 and Fig. 9 demonstrate the strong generalization capabilities
of our model across different datasets, with consistent high values for
accuracies and AUCs.
