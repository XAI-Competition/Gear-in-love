Abstract
Accurate fault classification in rotating machinery under changing speeds and loads is a critical challenge in industrial predictive maintenance, where vibration signatures shift across operating regimes and black-box decisions are difficult to trust. This paper presents a hybrid architecture that combines Transformers with Logic Tensor Networks (LTNs), used here as the neuro-symbolic learning framework because they ground first-order rules into differentiable satisfiability terms optimized directly in the training objective, for fault diagnosis on two public benchmarks: the Drivetrain Dynamics Simulator (DDS) (multiple speed/load regimes) and the University of Connecticut (UoC) gear-fault dataset. A compact 1-D Transformer encodes raw vibration windows, and an LTN layer imposes soft first-order constraints during training. We introduce a dynamic rule module that induces, merges, and prunes centroid-based similarity rules as the embedding geometry evolves, enabling the constraint set to adapt to within-class variability. Unlike prior LTN-based approaches such as LogicLSTM, which reweight a fixed rule set, our rules are induced and updated dynamically during training.
Experiments show improvements over strong neural and neuro-symbolic baselines on DDS (average accuracy 94.01% vs 88.20%), and gains over a strong Transformer baseline on UoC (macro F1 0.939). Beyond accuracy, the induced rules provide compact, queryable explanations by identifying prototypical vibration-window patterns that support a prediction. Confidence calibration improves versus baselines under the same evaluation protocol. Because LTN supervision acts only during training, inference latency matches the base Transformer. The results support neuro-symbolic fusion as a practical path to accurate and explainable fault diagnosis under varying operating conditions.

1. Introduction
Rotating machinery is widespread in industrial systems, and effective fault diagnosis is crucial for safety, reliability, and cost reduction [1], [2]. Traditionally, fault detection relied on vibration-signal analysis and expert knowledge [3]. Techniques such as spectral analysis, envelope detection, and wavelet transforms have been used to identify characteristic fault frequencies in bearings and gears [4], [5]. Early systems employed rule-based reasoning or fuzzy logic to encode diagnostic expertise [6], but these approaches suffered from scalability issues typical of the expert-systems era. Classical supervised learning techniques such as support-vector machines and shallow neural networks, were later applied to vibration features, yet still required significant manual feature extraction and did not scale well to complex or high-dimensional data [7], [8]. In recent decades, motivated by the limitations of earlier methods and by increased computational power and data availability, research has shifted to data-driven deep learning approaches that have substantially improved rotating machinery fault diagnosis [9], [10].
The rise of deep learning enabled end-to-end fault diagnosis directly from raw signals. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and autoencoders automatically learn discriminative features from vibration time series, outperforming models with manual feature engineering [11], [12], [13]. Researchers have applied deep CNNs [14], deep belief networks, and recurrent models to bearing and gearbox datasets with high accuracy [9]. These deep models capture complex patterns and have been extended to handle variable speeds and loads via data augmentation and domain adaptation [15], [16], [17]. Surveys affirm to the transformative impact of deep learning on fault diagnosis [18], [19], [20]. However, purely data-driven models act largely as black boxes and cannot easily incorporate prior engineering knowledge or reasoning about fault conditions [21]. This lack of interpretability and limited robustness under changing operating conditions motivates exploring hybrid intelligent systems [22], [23]. In safety–critical industrial applications, the inability to explain a model’s predictions undermines user trust and hinders adoption [24]. Moreover, models trained on data from a single machine or operating regime often fail to generalize when faced with new speeds, loads, or fault types without extensive retraining [25].
One practical reason is that vibration patterns reflect how the fault excites the machine. The measured signal is typically a mix of characteristic frequency components (such as shaft and gear-mesh-related content and their sidebands) and short defect impacts that excite structural resonance. When speed and load change, these components shift in frequency and relative energy, so the same fault can appear in multiple, physically plausible ways across operating regimes.
This highlights a concrete research gap. Many methods address operating-condition variability either by relying on model capacity and training protocol, or by injecting fixed priors (such as predefined rule sets or static constraints). Yet fixed knowledge cannot be updated when the data reveal new within-class regimes or when previously learned patterns become less relevant: it may regularize confidence, but it cannot revise the content of the constraints to track evolving fault signatures. These challenges have raised interest in combining data-driven learning with expert knowledge to enhance interpretability and adaptability [26].
Neuro-symbolic learning offers a compelling approach by integrating statistical learning with symbolic reasoning [27]. In the context of fault diagnosis, a neuro-symbolic framework can learn from raw sensor data while enforcing logical constraints derived from domain expertise (e.g., known causal relations or failure rules), potentially improving accuracy under limited or noisy data and providing explanations for model decisions [28], [29], [30], [31]. However, many neuro-symbolic diagnosis systems still assume a rule base specified a priori and kept fixed during training, which limits their ability to reflect regime-driven variability within a fault class.
The approach presented in this paper follows this paradigm. We propose a hybrid model that combines a Transformer-based neural network [32] with Logic Tensor Networks (LTNs) [22], [23] to perform fault classification in rotating machinery. The Transformer component handles low-level feature extraction from time-series signals, taking advantage of self-attention to capture long-range dependencies in vibration data [32]. On top of this, the LTN component injects high-level reasoning by encoding domain-specific logical rules (e.g., relationships between observed symptoms and fault modes) into the learning process. We do not treat the symbolic knowledge as static: we introduce a training-time dynamic rule module that induces, merges, and prunes prototype-grounded similarity rules as the embedding geometry evolves, so that the constraint set adapts to the regime structure discovered in the vibration data. This makes our model to learn a data-driven representation of machine condition and simultaneously reasons about faults in a human-understandable way.
This work makes the following contributions:
•
Hybrid Transformer–LTN diagnosis model: a compact 1-D Transformer guided by LTN supervision during training, with no inference-time overhead compared to the base Transformer.
•
Dynamic knowledge adaptation: a rule-induction and maintenance mechanism (merge/prune) that maintains a low-cardinality set of prototype-grounded similarity implications, enabling the constraint set to adapt under operating-condition variability.
•
Mechanically grounded link: an interpretable connection between induced prototypes and recurring vibration regimes, consistent with classical fault mechanisms.
•
Comprehensive evaluation and diagnostics: experiments on DDS and UoC, including accuracy/macro-F1, confidence calibration (ECE), and rule-linked explanation diagnostics that quantify when predictions are supported by the learned rule base.
The remainder of this paper reviews related work in data-driven fault diagnosis, neuro-symbolic AI, and information fusion methods, positioning our method within the state of the art and validating it on two benchmarks: the Drivetrain Dynamics Simulator (DDS) [33] and the University of Connecticut (UoC) gear fault datasets [34].
2. Related work
Recent hybrid methods in fault diagnosis have begun to explore this neuro-symbolic direction, but they typically rely on a fixed rule base that remains static during training. For example, LogicLSTM [33] augments an LSTM classifier with logical constraints, and convolutional neuro-fuzzy networks [35] embed expert fuzzy rules into a deep model. Similar designs include the Bayesian-Rule CNN [36] and the Self-Explainable TCN [37], both encode prior knowledge through immutable clauses whose truth values are merely reweighted. Empirical studies consistently report that such static knowledge bases generalize poorly when machines operate under unseen speeds, loads, or evolving fault signatures [24], [25], [38]. Deep Expert Network as well uses fixed signal-processing and logical-operator nodes to ensure interpretability [24]; however, its semantic structure is likewise frozen a priori.

We address these limitations by introducing a dynamic, training-time rule-generation mechanism combined with a generalized similarity rule which serves as a form of self-supervised regularization. In our framework, the knowledge base is no longer static: new logical constraints are added, merged, or removed on-the-fly as the latent feature space evolves, echoing the evolving-rule philosophy of Shah and Wang [39]. For instance, if the Transformer uncovers a new cluster of abnormal vibration patterns, the system can automatically formulate a rule to represent that emergent fault signature. In contrast, if certain fault signatures converge or become obsolete under different operating conditions, the corresponding rules are pruned.
Existing research on intelligent fault diagnosis in rotating machinery spans advanced deep learning architectures and neuro-symbolic techniques. Key strands include: (i) Transformer-based time-series models, (ii) hybrid CNN-and-Transformer architectures enriched with external knowledge, (iii) neuro-symbolic and other hybrid AI paradigms that inject symbolic structure into learning, (iv) transfer learning and domain/test-time adaptation methods for cross-condition diagnosis under distribution shift, and (v) graph and information-fusion frameworks that model multi-sensor or multi-view relationships in the data, often under label scarcity and varying operating conditions. A common practical driver across these strands is operating-condition variability: changes in speed and load shift characteristic harmonics, modulation sidebands, and impact patterns, so that a single fault class can manifest as multiple physically plausible regimes. Such regime variability is often seen in manufacturing assets due to changing duty cycles, production recipes, and maintenance states, so a proper diagnosis model has to remain reliable even when the same fault expresses differently across operating points.
Transformer variants have become state of the art for vibration-signal analysis, capturing long-range temporal patterns that CNN/LSTM backbones miss. Time-Series Transformer (TST) outperforms CNN/LSTM on bearing benchmarks [32]; subsequent CNN–Transformer hybrids improve robustness to variable loads [40], [41]. Very compact self-attention designs such as Synthesizer [42], Diagnosisformer [41], Siamese Transformers for few-shot scenarios [43], and knowledge-enhanced variants that inject physical priors or graph relationships [44], [45] further demonstrate the architecture’s flexibility. These models, however, are predominantly data-driven. Robustness under regime changes is typically achieved through representation capacity and training protocol, rather than through explicit constraints whose content can be revised as vibration signatures evolve.
Beyond raw attention, several works couple Transformers with explicit knowledge sources to boost interpretability and adaptability. Transparent Information Fusion Networks fuse multi-sensor features with self-organizing neuro-symbolic nodes [46], while large language model frameworks textualise vibration segments so reasoning can be carried out in natural language space [47]. These trends indicate a shift towards knowledge-guided Transformers for industrial diagnostics. This injected knowledge is often encoded as a fixed prior, graph, or auxiliary structure, rather than as a compact rule base that can be maintained and revised online as regimes and fault signatures shift.
Outside the Transformer backbone, hybrid reasoning has broadened. Evolving neuro-fuzzy classifiers remain popular [39], but very recent relational Neuro-Symbolic Markov Models enforce first-order constraints along the whole time axis [48]. Hierarchical Decision Transformers that interface with symbolic planners achieve long-horizon control under explicit logical goals [49], and Logic-LM style systems combine large language models with external solvers for faithful rule satisfaction [50]. Pseudo-semantic losses further provide gradient-based proxies that impose logic consistency on autoregressive Transformers [51]. These developments strengthen the case for explicit constraints, but they do not directly resolve the rotating-machinery setting where the relevant constraint set itself should evolve with speed/load-induced changes in the signal.
A closely related strand is transfer learning and domain/test-time adaptation for cross-condition diagnosis, which tackles distribution shift by aligning representations across operating regimes and updating the model as conditions evolve. Authors in [52] propose multi-source information transfer learning with subdomain adaptation to reduce cross-domain mismatch beyond global alignment. Moving to online settings, Tian et al. [53] introduce continual test-time domain adaptation under dynamic operating conditions, while Tian et al. [54] propose an evidential deep learning test-time adaptation strategy that stabilizes online updates via uncertainty-aware modelling.
Graph and information-fusion methods construct graphs over multi-sensor or multi-view features and learn fault representations with graph neural networks. A universal graph neural network with multiple strategy constraints learns unsupervised fault features from full-dimensional signal descriptions and yields embeddings that transfer across several industrial datasets [55]. A sample-level multi-representation fusion scheme with semi-supervised graph learning is proposed for rotating machinery fault diagnosis when only a small number of labelled samples are available [56]. At a more general representation-learning level, uncertainty-aware embeddings of attributed networks combine several feature views while preserving multi-order proximity in the underlying graph [57]. Multi-sensor information fusion via several non-Euclidean graphs over sensors and time has been applied to extract equipment features for multiple operation and maintenance tasks under limited task labels [58].
Further work uses graph structure and multi-sensor fusion for machinery health monitoring. Trusted multi-source information fusion with a modified graph convolution network has been reported for electromechanical systems [59]. Noise-robust multi-view graph neural networks have been proposed for rotating machinery fault diagnosis [60], and multi-view graph convolutional networks have been designed to fuse multi-source information for mechanical fault diagnosis [61]. Richly connected spatio-temporal graphs and attention-aware temporal–spatial graphs have been used to fuse multi-sensor signals for rotating machinery fault diagnosis [62], [63], while a dynamic graph attention network combined with time–frequency data fusion has been proposed for multi-sensor fault diagnosis [64]. Graph structures embedded with physical constraints have also been used in information-fusion networks for interpretable aero-engine fault diagnosis [65]. A recent survey reviews graph neural network-based fault-diagnosis methods across several industrial domains and synthesizes their main modelling patterns [66]. Together, these works highlight graph construction and multi-sensor fusion as powerful tools for robust diagnosis under label scarcity and varying operating conditions. However, the inductive bias is primarily expressed through topology and message passing, whereas our goal is a compact, explicit, first-order rule set whose content can be maintained and revised as the latent fault structure reorganizes under regime changes. Our approach is complementary: instead of encoding inductive bias through an explicit graph topology, we retain a compact 1D Transformer on raw vibration windows and impose structure via logical rules that are induced and updated during training, yielding explicit, queryable explanations.
Existing Transformer-based models for fault diagnosis rarely embed dynamic symbolic constraints, while most neuro-symbolic systems assume a fixed rule base. In rotating machinery, this becomes especially limiting under speed/load variability: the same fault can produce different mixtures of impulses, resonance ring-down, and load-dependent modulation sidebands across operating points, creating intra-class regimes that drift in prevalence and separation. Static constraints, like the label-consistency axioms, can regularize confidence but cannot represent and maintain this evolving multi-modality. By contrast, our model couples a lightweight Transformer encoder with Logic Tensor Networks whose similarity rules are continuously induced, merged, and pruned as training progresses. This induces a low-cardinality set of prototype-grounded implications that tracks the evolving embedding geometry, providing an explicit knowledge-adaptation mechanism aligned with operating-condition drift while preserving baseline Transformer inference latency. Our work unifies these two research lines: a lightweight Transformer guided by Logic Tensor Networks whose rules are continuously induced and pruned, thereby addressing the open problem stated as our research gap, that of knowledge adaptation under changing scenarios.
This neuro-symbolic diagnosis system is novel for rotating machinery: prior purely neural approaches have not incorporated formal logical reasoning, and traditional expert systems lack state-of-the-art deep feature extractors. By dynamically integrating learned signal representations with evolving symbolic rules, our contribution bridges this gap. The proposed model satisfies logical consistency constraints while learning rich features from data, thereby advancing both explainability and fault coverage. In summary, neuro-symbolic learning has the potential to enhance fault diagnosis by combining powerful feature learning [67] with domain knowledge and reasoning [26].

4. Experimental evaluation
We evaluate the proposed method on industrial vibration data. Experiments were run on Windows with 128 GiB RAM, an NVIDIA A4000 GPU, and an Intel(R) Core(TM) i9-10900X CPU.
Datasets. We use two benchmarks: the Drivetrain Dynamics Simulator (DDS) and the University of Connecticut (UoC) gear-fault datasets. Both datasets contain nine classes, but their fault taxonomies differ (Table 4, Table 5). In both cases we cast diagnosis as a supervised nine-way classification problem: short time-domain vibration windows are used as inputs, and the corresponding physical condition of the gearbox/bearing (healthy or specific fault type) provides the class label. Abbreviations in figures default to DDS shorthands (Table 4); for UoC, SPL denotes spall and CTF-1…CTF-5 denote chipping severity levels.

Train/test splits use balanced splits of 5000 training and 1000 test samples per fault type and speed, standardization, one-hot encoding of the nine classes, and generation of subsequences with a sliding window. For each fixed speed–load regime and for each of the nine DDS fault classes in Table 4, we first form a long, continuous vibration record per operating condition. From that record we extract windows of length

We then randomly subsample these windows without replacement to obtain balanced train and test sets with the same number of windows per class and regime, consistent with this configuration. This procedure is repeated independently for all speed–load regimes, so every domain in Table 9 is class-balanced at the window level. No synthetic signals are generated: all windows are cropped from the original DDS vibration recordings. The only source of noise is the measurement noise present in the test rig (mechanical and sensor noise); we do not add artificial white noise or other perturbations.
Standardization on DDS is performed via feature-wise z-score scaling: all feature columns (vibration samples and, when present, auxiliary channels) are treated as inputs, while the column for the fault type is kept as the discrete target. The scaling parameters (mean and standard deviation per feature) are estimated on the training portion of the data and then reused to transform the corresponding test portion.
UoC. Collected at the University of Connecticut [34], this dataset comprises nine gear conditions (Table 5) at 20 kHz. We use the benchmark’s preprocessed time-domain matrix, where each row is a univariate acceleration trace of length 936 samples (approximately 46.8 ms at 20 kHz). The matrix contains 3600 labelled traces, exactly 400 per fault condition, so that the class priors are perfectly balanced across the nine gear states. We do not relabel or re-sample the original runs and only discard a small number of corrupted records. Preprocessing (standardization and windowing) follows the common pipeline described below, and each resulting UoC window is a standardized univariate signal. This sparse, low-overlap windowing strategy mimics a realistic industrial scenario in which only short snippets of vibration data are available, while still covering each trace with multiple diagnostic snippets. Measurement noise from the experimental setup is naturally present in the traces. As it was the case with DDS, we do not add synthetic noise or perform any further data augmentation on UoC.

4.2.1. Rule-base dynamics under fixed and variable regimes
The DDS benchmark provides multiple operating regimes at different speeds and loads, which makes it a good testbed for inspecting how the induced rule base evolves during training. For each regime and cross-validation fold we therefore analyse the recorded number of similarity rules as a function of training epoch, together with the corresponding satisfiability and validation accuracy.
Fig. 3a illustrates the dynamics for the 20 Hz, 0 N m case. At the beginning of training the model starts with one similarity prototype per class (9 rules). As the Transformer embedding improves, additional sub-clusters are discovered and the number of rules increases. In the second half of training, merge and prune operations balance one another and the rule count fluctuates only slightly around a stable value, while both satisfiability and validation accuracy rise and then level off. This scenario is relatively easy (Table 9 reports an accuracy of around 90%), and the rule base behaves as expected: early exploratory growth followed by a compact, stable configuration.
A more demanding regime, 30 Hz at 1 N m, is shown in Fig. 3(b). Here the rule base again starts close to 9 rules but grows more rapidly. Subsequently, pruning removes weakly supported or redundant prototypes and the rule count contracts towards a plateau of around 10 rules. Validation accuracy is lower and more variable than in the previous case, and satisfiability displays modest oscillations, but the number of rules remains bounded and stabilizes in the later epochs. This scenario illustrates that, even when the classification problem is harder, the merge/prune mechanism prevents unbounded growth of the rule base and steers it towards a compact set of similarity rules.
We then consider the reported variable-speed/load tests, which mix operating conditions. For each variable-speed experiment we repeat the same analysis and summarize the resulting statistics in Fig. 3(c). Across all ten regimes the final number of rules lies in a narrowband (between 9 and 10 rules, with a mean of 9.3), and the mean rule count over the last 20% of epochs remains close to this final value (between 9.0 and 10.18 rules) with a small standard deviation (on average 0.22, never exceeding 0.39). Based on these observations, we argue that the dynamic rule module adapts to different speed/load combinations while keeping the rule base compact and temporally stable once training has converged.

In deployment, the runtime component is the base 1-D Transformer. LTN supervision and similarity-rule induction are applied only during offline training. This enables two complementary integration modes. Short-term. When a new operating point is introduced, the system can run a lightweight batch update: collect a small buffer of recent windows, recompute prototypes and refresh the similarity-rule set by rerunning Algorithm 1 on the batch (merge/prune included), and fine-tune the model for a small number of epochs before redeployment. During this cycle, the induced rules provide a compact diagnostic summary of what the model considers the new within-class regimes. Long-term. Under slowly evolving conditions, updates can be scheduled periodically using larger batches that span multiple regimes. In this setting, the rule base can be treated as an explicit, versioned diagnostic artefact. Rules and prototypes are stored alongside the model weights, reviewed for stability and coverage, and compared across update cycles to track emerging or disappearing regimes. This supports a strong documentation of regime changes, and targeted data acquisition for weakly supported modes.

5. Explainability discussion
The integration of symbolic rules into our hybrid Transformer-LTN improves both interpretability and reliability. By embedding domain knowledge as soft first-order constraints (Section 3.1), the model is trained not only to classify but also to respect known relations between fault modes and vibration signatures. All analyses below are computed on the test split of the UoC dataset (macro-F1, as reported in Table 7, is 0.939). On DDS, complementary physics-linked analyses of rule dynamics and attention in challenging regimes are reported in Section 4, in particular Sections 4.2.1 Rule-base dynamics under fixed and variable regimes, 4.2.2 Sequential operating-condition drift, 4.2.3 Window size study, where we relate the behaviour of the dynamic rule base and attention patterns to operating-condition changes and shaft-frequency harmonics.
Explainable by design: we consider our model to be explainable by design, because the similarity predicates and their rule implications are optimized during training (Section 3.1), their truth degrees can be evaluated for any new sample and reported as intrinsic explanation scores (predicate activations, rule satisfactions, implication truth). The LTN grounding further enables simple logical queries and automatic consistency checks against the current rule set. Moreover, by optimizing a satisfiability objective (Eq. (3)) in addition to, and opposed to the classical cross entropy, the learner is explicitly encouraged to concentrate probability where the rules hold, yielding faithful, queryable explanations and supporting basic deductive reasoning over the learned predicates.

6. Conclusion
We introduced a neuro-symbolic framework that couples a lightweight 1-D Transformer with Logic Tensor Networks to introduce domain rules into training while preserving the efficiency of a compact encoder at inference time. On two benchmarks, UoC and DDS, the hybrid model improves accuracy over strong baselines and provides rule-grounded diagnostics that make its decisions transparent.
In UoC, the class-to-class rule-conditioned view shows that, when a class rule is active, about 94% of the probability mass remains in the same class, with a small “confidence” (
≤
4
%
) to others. Rule–classifier consistency averages 0.55, and calibration is strong (ECE 0.0103, with a baseline comparison in Table 8). Attention rollout aligns with rule sensitivity at the patch level with a positive median RA, indicating that attended segments generally support the active rule. These results suggest that the classifier concentrates probability where the rules hold and that its confidence estimates are reliable.
On DDS, the model attains an average accuracy of 94.01%, improving over LogicLSTM (88.20%) and outperforming or matching other neural baselines across most operating regimes. Because LTN supervision acts only during training, the inference latency remains that of the base Transformer.
Combining similarity and label based rules with attention based features yields a classifier that is accurate and explainable on UoC and DDS. The rule set clarifies when predictions are supported by the learned similarity structure and when they are not, surfacing cases for targeted refinement (for example prototype augmentation or rule reweighting). Future work will integrate explicit engineering knowledge into the rule set and assess robustness under broader operating shifts and sensor configurations. The non-optimal DDS regimes discussed indicate that remaining errors are concentrated in operating regions with overlapping fault spectra and only moderate rule–classifier agreement, motivating future extensions with multi-scale temporal context, multi-sensor similarity predicates and regime-aware rule weighting.
